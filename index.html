Patient Psychological Debrief

Tybalt exemplifies typical lack of impulse control and emotional dysregulation. This patient experiences frequent and violent public outbursts, specifically against members of the house of Montague. These behaviors indicate an inability for self-control and perhaps “Intermittent Explosive Disorder” (IED).

“(IED) …involves repeated, sudden bouts of impulsive, aggressive, violent behavior or angry verbal outbursts. The reactions are too extreme for the situation” (- Mayo Clinic).

These actions have resulted in several serious injuries, potential deaths, and countless public disturbances. As for the root of the problems, the ongoing conflict between the House of Capulet (Tybalt’s family) and the House of Montague. Tybalt's dialogue and actions indicate loyalty to the Capulet family, and thus hate for the Montagues (perhaps a disproportionate amount). Tybalt's explosive personality combined with stressors from the conflict between families will eventually result in fatality if nothing is done.

Shakespeare characterizes Tybalt as the most fiery and violent character in the play. He uses this personality as the catalyst for dramatic moments in the play that become key plot points. For example, Tybalt's continued aggression in a confrontation with Romeo and friends leads to the death of Mercutio and Tybalt, as well as Romeo’s exile.

“Romeo:... Gentleman, for shame! Forbear this outrage! …Hold, Tybalt!...[Tybalt under Romeo’s arm thrusts Mercutio in…]” (Shakespeare, 428).

Despite Romeo stepping between them to break up the fight, Tybalt thrusts and kills Mercutio where he cannot fight back. This shows his anger and lack of impulse control in the play. He also wanted to kill Romeo at the Capulet party, but was restrained, only further building his anger later in the play.

Core Conflict Identification

The Primary Conflict

Tybalt’s primary external conflict is his man vs. man type conflict with the house of Montague, Romeo especially. His identity revolves around hatred of the Montagues; He hates Romeo specifically because he attended the Capulet party and Tybalt was not allowed to slay him. At every encounter with the Montagues, he attempts to start a fight.

Case Evidence

For example, when meeting servants of the Montagues and Benvolio in the street, he says,

“What, art thou drawn among these heartless hinds? Turn thee, Benvolio; look upon thy death” (Shakespeare, 379).

Without knowing the entirety of the situation, Tybalt already looks for a fight, showing his aggression and motivation against the Montagues.

Dramatic Function Analysis

Shakespeare creates Tybalt’s personality to be the spark to ignite fights and create conflict within the tragedy. It is Tybalt who, after killing Mercutio, is slain by Romeo, creating key plot points to drive the story forward.

“Romeo… Hold, Tybalt! Good Mercutio! [Tybalt under Romeo’s arm thrusts Mercutio in, and flies]” (Shakespeare, 428).

Killing Mercutio while he couldn’t defend both shows his impulsive, angry character and eventually results in Romeo's exile. The exile is what results in miscommunication and the tragic death of Romeo and Juliet. So, Tybalt is intentionally made by Shakespeare to have anger issues and be violent. It is these traits that cause the story to end the way it did and created tension throughout.

Session Notes: Glows and Grows

GLOW

Fierce Loyalty

Patient displays great loyalty to the Capulet family.

“This, by his voice, should be a Montague…Now, by the stock and honor of my kin, to strike him dead I hold it not a sin” (Shakespeare 1.5.54.58-59).
Clinical Analysis:
Tybalt is portrayed as a merciless character who is hostile toward the Montagues. Shakespeare uses this to emphasize the dramatic irony of Romeo’s marriage to Juliet as a way to challenge the views of Tybalt and highlight the cause of his downfall. Tybalt remained loyal to the Capulet family even until his death, earning him great honor and the great grief of his family.
GROW

Anger Management

Patient displays violent tendencies and has trouble managing anger.

“Patience perforce with willful choler meeting makes my flesh tremble in their different greeting. I will withdraw; but this intrusion shall, now seeming sweet, convert to bitt’rest gall” (Shakespeare 1.5.89-92).
Clinical Analysis:
Shakespeare foreshadows the tragedy that will occur in the story that further develops the play. Tybalt expresses his intense rage toward Capulet forcing him to suppress his urge to kill Romeo, displaying him as a violent and fierce character.

Patient Treatment Plan

The patient has a strong sense of justice and loyalty toward the Capulet house; however, the patient struggles with controlling emotions of anger and resentment. He refuses to accept and forgive Romeo, admitting to him that

“the love I bear thee can afford no better term than this: thou art a villain” (Shakespeare 3.1.57-58).

Shakespeare uses dialogue and tone to emphasize Tybalt’s function as a catalyst for the main conflict of the story, with his aggressive tendencies leading to Romeo’s banishment, the greater divide between the families, and the highlight of the play’s shift from a love story to a tragedy.

  • 1
    Exposure & Acceptance: If possible, Romeo will confess his marriage to Juliet, and Tybalt is to find acceptance of their marriage and Romeo himself.
  • 2
    Conflict Resolution: The patient is to attempt to resolve the differences between the two parties and find peace for the two.
  • 3
    Empathy Building: He is also to spend more time with Romeo in order to see his true character and heal the hatred toward him.
  • 4
    Re-channeling Loyalty: In favor and utilization of his loyalty, he will turn to find new loyalty for both families instead of the Capulets alone.

Results: Final Assessment

Tybalt was characterized by Shakespeare to have unwavering loyalty towards Capulets, but having the extreme opposite towards the Montagues. His family pride and loyalty reinforces his hatred towards the Montagues as with the Capulet’s alignment.

Capulet wants Tybalt to

“take no note of him. It is my will, which if thou respect… It fits, when such a villain is a guest. I’ll not endure him” (1. 5. 71-76).

Just a moment ago, his intent to kill Romeo was instinct; however, his murderous intent was instantly dismissed by Lord Capulet.

This stresses his family loyalty as his devious intent was quickly stopped when confronted by Lord Montague/Capulet. In addition, his hate for the Montagues is also shown as he still remarks Romeo a “villain”.

Conclusion

Thus, Tybalt is a prime example of family loyalty and is emphasized through his hate and violence towards the Montagues.
